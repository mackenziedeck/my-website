Fish Identification

Atlantic Salmon

These fish can be hard to identify and can be confused for chinook, coho, or rainbow trout. Adults have black spots on sides, dark pectoral fins, and forked caudal fin. They have two dorsal fins and one adipose fin. The jaw typically extends to the back edge of the eye or further. Atlantic salmon are known for their leaping and fighting ability

Largemouth Bass

They have two dorsal fins and a long body. A good way to identify them is if the upper jaw extends past the eye. They are light green with a dark lateral stripe. In Michigan, largemouth bass are not typically found deeper than 20 feet. They are most active in warm waters, about 70-85 degrees.

Smallmouth Bass

Very similar to the largemouth bass but the jaw only extends to the middle of the eye. They have dark vertical bars. The usual smallmouth is 8 to 15 inches long and weighs less than three pounds, but they are capable of reaching up to six pounds.

Bluegill

They have two spiny dorsal fins that look like one dorsal fin. They have small mouths. They have a unique faint black coloring near the back of the dorsal fin which helps separate them from other sunfish.

Brook Trout

The Brook Trout is native to Michigan and is the state fish. Brook Trout from the Great Lakes can reach up to 25 inches long. They have a long body with a mouth that extends past the eye. They have multiple colors green, blue gray, and black with a silver belly and a vermiculation pattern. They also sometimes have red and blue spots on their sides.

Brown Trout

Brown trout have a brown or yellowish back that fades to a lighter color toward the belly. They have large dark spots with pale or red halos on their sides and back. Typically, the tail is plain or has only a few spots. However, Brown Trout in the great lakes have a more silver color and may not have spots.

Bullhead

Brown bullheads have black or yellow-brown barbels on the chin and watch out for the saw-like barbs on the pelvic spines. Black bullheads are more dark green or black with a pale belly. Their fin spines are sharp but not saw-like.

Channel Catfish

They have a very forked tail like a 'v' shape. They have whiskers or barbels around the mouth. Channel cats are very slender with a pale speckled body. The best time to go catfishing is at night.

Chinook Salmon

They can be a silvery color or darker depending on when they spawn. They also have large black spots on the back and upper tail. They can typically weigh 20-30 pounds.

Coho Salmon

Coho can have a dark green or blue color on their back with silver sides. They have small dark spots on the back. The typical adult weighs 5 pounds.

Suckers and Carp

Suckers have no barbels and a long cylinder shape body. They can have small scales depending on the type. Carp have small barbels on the corners of their mouths with a triangle shaped head. Their fins don't have spines and they have large scales on their body.

Crappie

A panfish species that is fun to catch. Black crappie have random dark spots and White crappie usually have vertical stripes.

Flathead Catfish

They can be dark brown or yellow in color. They do not have a forked tail. Look for a protruding lower jaw. They like to live in logs or other structures.

Lake Sturgeon

The Lake Sturgeon is a Michigan native fish. Lake Sturgeon have faced near extinction in the past and the state takes many efforts to restore the population. A Lake Sturgeon being harvested must be registered and is limited to one per year per angler only from specific waters. Lake Sturgeon are large bony fish. They have a large sucker like mouth with barbels and a single dorsal fin near the tail. The current state record for a harvested Lake Sturgeon is 193 pounds!

Lake Trout

Another Michigan native that has been threatened in the past, their population has made a come back. they are black or gray with light spots and orange red fins. The tail is forked. They prefer cold water and are can be found deep near the bottom of the Great Lakes.

Muskellunge

Often called 'the fish of 10,000 casts', Muskie are notoriously elusive. They are a member of the Pike family and look very similar. Look for a pointed forked tail and a vertical pattern on the body. Watch out for their sharp teeth!

Northern Pike

Pike are fierce ambush predators that will eat any living creature, even small mammals and ducks. They are usually a light yellow gold color and have horizontal bean shaped spots on the body. They have a more round forked tail compared to Muskies.

Pink Salmon

The ones found in lakes are more silver in color. They have large oval spots on the back. You can tell different salmons apart by the mouth color, Pink Salmon have a light colored mouth and dark tongue.

Pumpkinseed

A fish very similar to bluegill but they have horizontal aqua blue lines on their face. They have a vibrant yellow, bright orange, scarlet, and blue coloration and are typically taller than bluegill.

Rainbow Trout

They have small black spots along the body with a pink horizontal stripe down the middle. Rainbow Trout live inland, the large ones that live in the Great Lakes are actually Steelhead.

Rock Bass

Look for a large mouth, red eyes, and six spines on the bottom fin. These fish can be a variety of colors from gold brown to green. They can actually rapidly change color to match their surroundings.

Walleye

A fish native to Michigan, Walleye's are a member of the Perch family. They have two large dorsal fins, one is spiny. Large, reflective eyes and canine teeth are distinct features. They do not have vertical markings like Perch.

Perch

They have two dorsal fins, very similar to Walleye. Look for vertical bars and no canine teeth. Their average length is 4-10 inches.

Source:Michigan DNR
